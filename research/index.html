  • Gender, Risk Aversion, and Travel: How COVID-19 Affected Participation in Ultramarathons
    • Many running events have been cancelled during the COVID-19 era, yet many others have continued. By using a novel data set containing information on ultramarathon races (long distance or long time races comprising a popular form of outdoor recreation) and their participants, we study the decision to compete in a race during the COVID-19 era. By restricting our analysis to those runners who participated in the pre-COVID-19 version of a race and observing whether or not they attended that same event during the COVID-19 era (or if they attended another ultramarathon within $\pm$ 1 month of the expected event date for cancelled events) we are able to estimate the impact of local and race destination COVID-19 cases on the decision to compete. We find that local COVID-19 cases, along with a series of expected socioeconomic factors including runner ability, runner experience, travel distance, education level, and gender all affect the decision to compete in a COVID-19 era ultramarathon. We also find that events were more likely to be cancelled when COVID-19 cases rose in the state in which the race is located. Women were also disproportionately affected by the pandemic when it comes to changes in travel behavior.
